# Old and New Tax Regime Calculator — Complete Comparison Guide for FY 2025-26

Picking the right tax regime is one of the most impactful financial decisions a salaried individual makes each year — and it's surprisingly easy to get wrong. The difference between the old and new regime isn't just about tax rates; it's about whether your deductions and exemptions outweigh the benefit of lower slabs. For FY 2025-26, with the new regime now the default under Section 115BAC, this decision deserves careful thought rather than a gut call. Running both scenarios through a reliable calculator before filing is the smartest starting point.

## Old Tax Regime vs New Tax Regime — Key Differences

Here's a side-by-side look at how the two regimes compare across the features that matter most:

| Feature | Old Regime | New Regime |
|---|---|---|
| Tax Slabs | Higher rates (5%, 20%, 30%) | Lower rates (5% to 30% across more bands) |
| Standard Deduction | ₹50,000 | ₹75,000 |
| HRA Exemption | Available | Not available |
| 80C Deduction | Up to ₹1,50,000 | Not available |
| 80D (Medical Insurance) | Up to ₹25,000–₹50,000 | Not available |
| NPS (80CCD 1B) | Additional ₹50,000 | Not available |
| Home Loan Interest (Sec 24B) | Up to ₹2,00,000 | Not available |
| Default Regime (FY 2025-26) | No — opt-in required | Yes — applies automatically |

The new regime strips away most deductions in exchange for lower slab rates and a higher standard deduction. Whether that trade-off works in your favour depends entirely on how much you currently claim.

## Who Should Choose the New Tax Regime?

The new regime works well for people who don't have a strong deduction story. Here are four situations where it clearly makes sense:

- **Income up to ₹15 lakh with minimal investments** — If you aren't maxing out 80C, don't have a home loan, and receive limited HRA, the lower slab rates under the new regime will almost certainly result in less tax. The income tax calculation in old and new regime often shows a significant gap at this bracket.
- **Young earners early in their careers** — Someone without a home loan, no dependants requiring medical coverage claims, and a simple salary structure is exactly who the new regime was designed for. Fewer deductions mean the new regime's slab advantage is fully realised.
- **Salaried employees relying only on standard deduction** — If ₹75,000 is the only deduction you're claiming anyway, there's little reason to stay in the old regime. The new regime gives you that same deduction at lower tax rates.
- **Those who prefer simplicity at filing time** — The new regime dramatically reduces paperwork and the need to track proofs. For people who find ITR filing stressful, this alone is a practical reason to consider it.

## Who Should Stick to the Old Tax Regime?

The old regime isn't obsolete — for the right profile, it still saves meaningful money:

- **Home loan holders with interest above ₹2 lakh** — Section 24(b) allows up to ₹2 lakh deduction on home loan interest for self-occupied property. Combined with 80C and HRA, someone paying a large EMI in a metro can stack deductions that easily outperform the new regime's lower slab benefit.
- **HRA claimants renting in major cities** — Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru, and Hyderabad rents are significant. If your HRA exemption works out to ₹1.5–2.5 lakh or more annually, the old and new regime tax calculator comparison will often tip in favour of staying put.
- **Disciplined 80C investors** — Anyone consistently putting ₹1.5 lakh into ELSS, PPF, LIC, or EPF contributions beyond employer deduction gets a sizable taxable income reduction under the old regime. For incomes between ₹10–20 lakh with this deduction stack, the old regime frequently wins.
- **NPS contributors using 80CCD(1B)** — The additional ₹50,000 NPS deduction under Section 80CCD(1B) is not available in the new regime. For those who actively use this, it's worth computing the new tax regime and old tax regime calculator output before making the switch.

## How to Compare Both Regimes Using a Calculator

Manual comparison isn't difficult, but it is tedious — especially once you factor in slab-by-slab tax, cess, and rebate eligibility. Here's a clean way to approach it:

1. Note your gross annual income and all deductions you currently claim
2. Calculate taxable income under the old regime (gross minus all deductions)
3. Apply old regime slab rates and compute tax including cess
4. For the new regime, subtract only the ₹75,000 standard deduction (if salaried), apply the new slabs, and check Section 87A eligibility
5. Compare the final figures side by side

## New Regime Tax Slabs FY 2025-26

The IT department updated the new regime slabs significantly for this financial year. Here's the full picture:

| Income Slab | Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to ₹4,00,000 | Nil |
| ₹4,00,001 – ₹8,00,000 | 5% |
| ₹8,00,001 – ₹12,00,000 | 10% |
| ₹12,00,001 – ₹16,00,000 | 15% |
| ₹16,00,001 – ₹20,00,000 | 20% |
| ₹20,00,001 – ₹24,00,000 | 25% |
| Above ₹24,00,000 | 30% |

> **Section 87A Rebate:** For salaried individuals, the ₹75,000 standard deduction brings the effective tax-free threshold to ₹12,75,000. The Section 87A rebate (up to ₹60,000) makes net tax liability zero for anyone with taxable income up to ₹12,00,000. A 4% health and education cess applies on all tax computed above this threshold.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1. Which tax regime is the default for FY 2025-26, and do I need to do anything to opt out?**
The new tax regime under Section 115BAC is the default for all individual taxpayers from FY 2024-25 onwards — this continues for FY 2025-26. If you want the old regime instead, you need to actively opt for it while filing your ITR. Salaried individuals can do this directly in the ITR form without any separate declaration. The IT Department's [official page for salaried individuals](https://www.incometax.gov.in/iec/foportal/help/individual/return-applicable-1) explains this process clearly, including the distinction between salaried and business income filers.

**Q2. Can I switch between the old and new regime every year? Does the rule differ for salaried and business taxpayers?**
Yes, but with an important difference. Salaried individuals and those without business income can switch between regimes every year simply by indicating their choice in the ITR — no separate form needed. Business owners and professionals, however, can only opt out of the new regime once in a lifetime using Form 10-IEA. After that, if they switch back to the new regime, they cannot return to the old one again. This makes the income tax calculator comparison old and new regime especially important for people with business income, since the decision is less reversible. For the full rules on Form 10-IEA and switching, the IT Department's [FAQ page on new vs old regime](https://www.incometax.gov.in/iec/foportal/help/new-tax-vs-old-tax-regime-faqs) covers this in detail.

**Q3. What happens to carry-forward losses if I switch to the new regime?**
This is a commonly overlooked issue. If you have carry-forward losses from house property (typically home loan interest losses set off against income), those cannot be set off against income in a year when you opt for the new regime. Loss from house property under the old regime can only be set off and carried forward within the old regime framework. Switching to the new regime effectively pauses that set-off benefit for that year.

**Q4. What's the actual in-hand salary difference between the two regimes for a ₹15 lakh CTC?**
For a ₹15 lakh gross salary, assuming the standard deduction only under the new regime, taxable income comes to ₹14.25 lakh. Tax works out to approximately ₹1,05,000 + 4% cess = ₹1,09,200. Under the old regime with typical deductions — ₹1.5L (80C) + ₹50,000 (NPS) + ₹25,000 (80D) = ₹2.25L in deductions on top of the ₹50,000 standard deduction — taxable income drops to ₹12.25L. Old regime tax on this is roughly ₹1,62,500, higher than the new regime. The itr new regime calculator and old regime path both confirm that at ₹15 lakh with modest deductions, the new regime typically wins.

**Q5. Do senior citizens benefit more from the old regime?**
Often yes, though it depends on their income sources. The old regime gives senior citizens (60–79 years) a higher basic exemption of ₹3 lakh and super senior citizens (80+) a ₹5 lakh exemption — neither benefit exists in the new regime, where the same ₹4 lakh basic exemption applies to everyone. For senior citizens with pension income, interest income, and significant 80C or 80D deductions, the old regime can result in considerably lower tax. The new income tax regime calculator is still worth running for this group, because at very high incomes the new regime's lower slab rates can flip the outcome.
