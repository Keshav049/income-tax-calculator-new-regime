# New Tax Regime Calculation — Complete Guide for FY 2025-26

This page walks you through exactly how income tax is calculated under the new regime for FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27). Whether you're a salaried employee trying to understand your payslip deductions or just planning ahead before filing, this covers the full picture — step by step, with real numbers.

## Step-by-Step: Income Tax Calculation as Per New Regime

Let's use a concrete example: a salaried individual earning **₹12,00,000 per year**.

**Step 1 — Start with Gross Salary**
Gross annual income = **₹12,00,000**

**Step 2 — Deduct the Standard Deduction**
Salaried employees get a flat standard deduction of ₹75,000 under the new regime.
₹12,00,000 − ₹75,000 = **₹11,25,000 (taxable income)**

**Step 3 — Apply the New Regime Tax Slabs**

| Slab | Amount Taxed | Rate | Tax |
|------|-------------|------|-----|
| ₹0 – ₹3,00,000 | ₹3,00,000 | Nil | ₹0 |
| ₹3,00,001 – ₹7,00,000 | ₹4,00,000 | 5% | ₹20,000 |
| ₹7,00,001 – ₹10,00,000 | ₹3,00,000 | 10% | ₹30,000 |
| ₹10,00,001 – ₹11,25,000 | ₹1,25,000 | 15% | ₹18,750 |

**Step 4 — Total Tax Before Rebate**
₹0 + ₹20,000 + ₹30,000 + ₹18,750 = **₹68,750**

**Step 5 — Check Section 87A Rebate**
Since the taxable income (₹11,25,000) is below ₹12,00,000, the full Section 87A rebate applies.
Tax after rebate = **₹0**

**Step 6 — Add 4% Health & Education Cess**
Cess on ₹0 = ₹0

**Final Tax Payable = ₹0**

This is why income tax calculation under the new regime has become genuinely attractive for a large chunk of the salaried middle class — the 87A rebate effectively wipes out tax liability for most people earning up to ₹12 lakh. For the official breakdown of how Section 87A rebate limits work across regimes, the [Income Tax Department's tax rates page](https://incometaxindia.gov.in/charts%20%20tables/tax%20rates.htm) has the full details.

## Common Mistakes When Calculating Tax Under New Regime

People get this wrong more often than you'd think. Here are the errors worth watching out for:

- **Skipping the standard deduction** — Many salaried taxpayers forget to deduct ₹75,000 before applying slabs, which results in a higher (and incorrect) tax figure. Freelancers and the self-employed do not get this deduction.
- **Applying 87A rebate incorrectly** — The rebate applies only if net taxable income (after standard deduction) is ₹12 lakh or below. If your income is ₹12,00,001, the rebate does not apply and the full slab-wise tax becomes payable.
- **Forgetting the 4% cess** — Tax calculators that skip cess give you a number that's 4% short. The health and education cess applies on the total income tax figure, not on your gross income.
- **Assuming all deductions still apply** — Under the new regime, 80C, 80D, HRA, and most other Chapter VI-A deductions are not available. The calculation of income tax under the new regime starts with gross income minus only the standard deduction.
- **Using old slab rates** — The FY 2025-26 slabs were revised. If you're referencing older material or an outdated tool, your numbers won't match what the department actually computes. The Income Tax Department's [guidance page for salaried individuals](https://www.incometax.gov.in/iec/foportal/help/individual/return-applicable-1) spells out how the default regime works and what it means for your ITR filing.

## Old Regime vs New Regime — Tax Calculation Comparison

The old regime allows deductions like 80C (up to ₹1.5L), 80D, home loan interest, and HRA — which can significantly reduce taxable income for people who actually use these. The new regime skips all of that and offers lower slab rates instead. For someone with ₹3–4 lakh in genuine deductions, the old regime can still come out cheaper. For everyone else — especially those without a home loan or heavy 80C investments — the income tax calculation old and new regime comparison almost always tips in favour of the new regime.

## FAQs on New Regime Tax Calculation

**Q1. Does the new regime apply differently to senior citizens?**
Senior citizens (age 60–79) and super senior citizens (80+) follow the same slabs under the new regime — there's no age-based slab relaxation like there is in the old regime. However, the Section 87A rebate and standard deduction of ₹75,000 apply to them as well if they're drawing a pension classified as salary income.

**Q2. How do freelancers calculate income tax for the new regime?**
Freelancers need to calculate income tax as per new regime on their net professional income — that is, gross receipts minus legitimate business expenses. The standard deduction of ₹75,000 is not available to them. Slab rates then apply on the remaining income, followed by cess and any applicable surcharge.

**Q3. What is the income limit for the 87A rebate when calculating tax on new regime?**
For FY 2025-26, the Section 87A rebate of up to ₹60,000 is available to resident individuals whose net taxable income does not exceed ₹12,00,000. This rebate effectively makes tax liability nil for anyone earning up to that threshold under the new regime.

**Q4. Which regime works out better for a ₹10 lakh salary?**
For a salaried individual with ₹10 lakh gross income, the income tax calculator for new regime shows zero tax payable (after standard deduction and 87A rebate). Under the old regime, you'd need deductions of roughly ₹2.5 lakh or more to match that outcome. For most people at this income level without significant 80C or home loan deductions, the new regime is the better call.

**Q5. Can HRA be claimed while calculating tax under the new regime?**
No. HRA exemption is not available under the new regime. If you receive HRA as part of your salary structure, it will be fully taxable. This is one of the key trade-offs — you get lower slab rates, but components like HRA, LTA, and most allowances lose their exempt status. The Income Tax Department's [official FAQ on new vs old tax regime](https://www.incometax.gov.in/iec/foportal/help/new-tax-vs-old-tax-regime-faqs) confirms this, along with answers to other common questions around deductions and regime switching.
